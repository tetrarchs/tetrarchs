Slavery in the Classical World: Week 3

 

BECOMING A SLAVE

 

 

 

Sources: Wiedemann Sourcebook chs. 2 and 6; Augustine, Letters 10* and 24* (in library under Classics LE 52); Jerome, Life of Malchus (in library under Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers vol. 6 [Ancient History Quartos X 65 Nic] or Penguin Classics Early Christian Lives [Ancient History X 65 Whi])

 

 

Questions to think about:

What are the different ways in which someone can become a slave?  In what ways do these vary in importance over antiquity?  What are the different ways in which someone can acquire a slave?  Why might someone acquire a slave?  What do we know of slave prices?  What do we know of the mechanics of the slave trade itself?

 

 

What problems are cause by ancient statistics or lack of them relating to slaves or population in general?  Does it matter?  How can modern demographic ideas be utilized?

 

 

 

Bibliography: Bradley 1994 ch. 3; Garlan in Finley 1987 (on piracy); Braund 1989; Finley 1981 ch. 11; Bradley in Finley 1987

For the Roman law of slave status and buying/selling slaves, see Buckland 1908 chs. 2-3, 17-18; more briefly in Watson 1987.

For current discussion of slave demography, see contrasting pair of articles by Scheidel 1997 and Harris 1999 (also Harris 1980; 1994).  For demography in general, see excellent introduction by Parkin 1992.  On Italian population, see Brunt 1971 (note list of census figures pp. 13-14 and App. 7)  and Lo Cascio (1994) 23-40.

On slave prices, see Scheidel 1996 (in teaching collection)
